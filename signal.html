This page highlights some useful signal processing algorithms that I've used for feature extraction in multiple projects. Since I cannot share the information about those work-related projects, I've used simulated data and example cases to show my understanding and implementation.

Fast Fourier Transform

  • The most common technique for extracting useful features from high speed waveforms, i.e. accelerometer sensors, proximity probes, acoustic or pressure signals, is using Fourier Analysis.
  • Fourier analysis is a mathematical technique to convert a signal from time domain to frequency domain by decomposing the signal in its periodic components.
  • Trasnforming the signal into frequency domain provides the capability to understand the individual frequencies that make up the signal.
  • The following figure shows an example of a vibration signal in the time domain for a 1-sec duration and at a high sampling rate.

  • The Fast Fourier Trasform of the acceleration signal is showin in the figure below, which shows the dominants frequencies with peaks in the frequency domain.
  • If the system information is known then certain frequencies of interest could be known in advance. For example, blade pass frequency for a rotating blade would be the number of blades times the speed in hertz, similarly ball pass frequency can be known for a ball bearing and the amplitudes at these frequencies can be useful indicators of any faults propagating in the component or system.
  • Another possibility is to track ceratin bands of interest in the frequence domain the use statistical calculations on the values within the band.

Empirical Mode Decomposition

  • Empirical mode decomposition is another method of decomposing the signal into separate components called Intrinsic Mode Functions (IMFs).
  • While FFT assumes that the input signal is periodic and a combination of sin waves, EMD does not rely on any such assumption and is purely based on the data.
  • The EMD algorithm works by identifying the local maxima and minima of the input signal and producing an upper and lower envelope of the signal. Further, the mean of the upper and lower envelope is calculated. The following image shows the input signal with its local maxmima and minima marked.

  • The mean is calculated based on the two envelopes shown by the red line in the figure above. The mean value so calculated is subtracted from the initial signal. This process is repeated until a given stopping criterion is met. When that happens, the first IMF is obtained. The next IMF is obtained by subracting the previously obtained IMF from the original signal and repeating the steps described above again. This continues until all the IMFs are extracted from ths signal and the residue contains no more than two maxima.

  • The individual IMFs obtained from the signal decomposition can be used to obtain additional features for health monitoring, provide valuable insights about the system at hand, or even be used for accurate forecasting of the original time series.

Order Analysis

  • Order analysis is a technique for extracting information about defects from a pair of promixity probes, typically installed 90 degrees apart on the housing, that measure the displacement of the shaft perpendicular to the axis of rotation within the housing.
  • A third proximity sensor is attached at location along the shaft that has a notch. This is the tachometer signal that is used to estimate the speed of the shaft since this signal captures the peaks whenever the notch crosses the proximity probe to identify a complete revolution.
  • The following figure shows an illustration of a typical proximity probe installation and an example of the signals captured by them.

  • One advantage of such a setup is that it provides the capability to view the spectral results from the displacement waveforms in a speed-normalized approach, that is known as the order domain. Since the frequencies of interest (1x, 2x, blade pass freq., ball pass freq.) that exhibit signatures of defects are mostly related to the shaft speed, therefore the peaks in the frequency domain will move as the speed changes.
  • However, in the order domain, such frequencies of interest are converted in terms of “orders”, which can be understood as the number of times “some event” repeats in a revolution and that stays constant regardless of the speed of the shaft.
  • The following figure gives an example for a signal at varying speeds and how the spectrum compares in the frequency domain to that in the order domain.

  • The procedure for order analysis involves converting the signal from the time domain to angle domain, i.e. the sampling of the displacement/vibration signal should be at constant angular increments rather than constant time increments.
  • This is achieved by a technique called order tracking, where the re-sampling of the original signal is done by assuming that angular acceleration with time, t, of the shaft angle, Ɵ, can be described by a quadratic equation: Ɵ(t) = b0 + b1t + b2t2. The tachometer signal provides angular reference for start and end of one shaft rotation which is used to solve for the coefficients in the quadratic equation.
  • Once the signals are converted to angle domain, either the spectral analysis of the signals could be done or the x and y signal could be plotted versus each other to create an orbit, which is a two-dimensional depiction of the machine's movements.
  • The shape of the orbit can provide useful information about various kinds of faults developing in the system.
